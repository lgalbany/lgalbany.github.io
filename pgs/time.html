7. BABILONIAN CALENDAR

 

In Babilonia and Mesopotamia different kinds of calendars coexisted until the Caldeans summarized them in one and spread it all over the country. This calendar was based on Lunar cycles, which lead to months of 28, 29 and 30 days and to years that had from 354 to 360d.

It made seasons were not synchronized with the calendar. That’s why they added a month called Embolismal in order to correct it. Later on we will see that many other cultures have used this method to correct the delay between lunar and solar years.

 

Finally, by rounding up the moon cycle, they established 12 months of 30 days in order to achieve a year of 360d, which permitted them to work with a sexagesimal base.

A legacy of this change is the sky division in 360º. An the 12 Zodiac constellations.

Another important remarkable thing and that will remain throughout time in the area is the fact that the day begins with the sunset.

 

8. EGIPTIAN CALENDAR

 

It is the first known Solar Calendar. It dates from 4236 BC.

Egiptians, basing on Chaldeans measurements, decided to divide years into 360 days. They realized that Nile’s flood followed a template a little bit longer than the Chaldean one. They decided to add 5 ‘rest’ days at the end of the year called Epagomens to honour the 5 most important gods, leading to years of 365 days.

 

This cycle began when Sirius, the most shining star, appeard parallel to the Sun. The Years had 3 seasons of 120 days:

-         AJET (flood season)

-         PERET (sow season)

-         SHEMU (harvest season)

The Egiptian Calendar repoduces the Earth revolution retarding 1y every 1460y.

 

In 238 BC Canopus tried to reform the calendar by adding one day every 4y, but he never succeeded.

 

9. ROMAN CALENDAR

 

At the same time, the Romans had their own calendar. It began exactly on what we would date as the 21st of April 753BC, the day of Rome’s foundation.

 

The Roman year is composed by 10 months of 30 or 31 days that sum 304 days. As it was too short, in the year 700 BC Pompilus reformed it and enlarged by adding two months: one at the beginning (nowadays February) and the other at the end (nowadays January). It changed the duration of the months and made a total of 355d as the lunar year.

 

Then, they used the Embolismal month in order to make the lunar year similar to the solar year.

 

In 450 BC Tenedos reformed the calendar again by adding the Embolismal month 3 times every 8 years. This reform approached the year duration to the tropic year.

 

10. JULIAN CALENDAR

 

In the year 46 BC Julius Cesar applied the Canopus reform to the Roman calendar and changed the beginning of the year and the duration of the month.

45 BC was a year of confusion: they had to sum all the delayed days since Rome’s foundation. This year had 445 days.

In addition, every 4 years a day was added. In those leap years there were two 23rd of February.

Julius was murdered in 44 BC and his name was given to a month in order to honour his memory. The same happened with the emperor Augustus.

In 321 AD Constantin imported the 7-day week of the Jewish, established that Sundays would be a free day and set all festive days in the calendar.

 

In 527 AD Jesus Christ’s birth was set 753 years after Rome foundation.

And in 607 AD they changed the origin of the year counting.

 

11. GREGORIAN CALENDAR

 

As I’ve mentioned, Constantin fixed all festive days in the calendar. As the Easter Sunday is based in the Jewish Calendar (which is Lunar), in the Julian calendar it was moving.

In 325 AD in the Nicea’s Council they decided that Easter would take place on the first Sunday after the first Full Moon after the Spring Equinox.

As the Julian Calendar loses 1 day every 128y, in 1500 AD it had already lost 11 days and the Easter Sunday had been delayed from the Equinox.

 

Greogrium wanted to correct this errors by fitting the calendar into the tropic year. He established that leap years would be the divisibles by 4 except those that finish in 00 and are not divisibles by 400. by doing this, they achieved an error of 1 day every 3323 years. To correct the accumulated looses they added 11 days at once, therefore, the 15th October 1582AD is followed by the 4th October 1582.

This is our calendar, an effort of various civilizations, reformed many times in order to achieve this tiny error.

20. WATCHES

 

Up to this moment, we have been referring to the relation between natural cycles and measuring of time. But we could still want to measure even shorter cycles.

Units into which such shorter cycles are divided have been decided by men and do not respond to any natural cycle.

 

Historically, many kinds of watches have been used:

-         Solar: it comes from Egipt. By using solar height and trajectory the day can be divided into different periods. At night they used a Water watch: a glass full of water with a hole in it through which the water went out and the amount of water left in the glass (height) would count the time.

-         Sand: the period of time it can measure depends on the amount of sand it has in it and the size of its hole.  

-         pendulus: depending on the mass of the pendulus and the length of its arm you can adjust it to make periods of one second.

-         Electronic: Uses some circuits called counters to divide the frequency of an oscillator to 1Hz, count seconds. We usually use quartz oscillators. But you can achieve an exact frequency using atomic vibration frequencies.

 

Untill 1967 we defined the second as the 86400th part of the average solar day between 1750 and 1890.

Nowadays the second is currently defined as the duration of 9,192,631,770 periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the ground state of the caesium-133 atom.

 

21. ATOMIC

 

This kind of wathes work due to MASERs, similar to lasers but emitting microwaves, by estimulated emissions.

The first one was made of amoniac and it was built in the USA in 1949. But nowadays are made of 133Cs which can achieve a delay of 1s every 30000y.

The most precise one is located in Paris and it is delayed 1s every 52 million years.

When a photon emitted by a 133Cs atom oscillates 9.192.631.770 times, 1 atomic second has passed.

 

22. GPS

 

One of the applications that these kind of watches have is the GPS one.

The GPS is a group of American satellites (Napstar GPS) situated at 20.000km of altitude distributed in order to cover all the surface of the Earth.

 

The First satellite was launched in 1978 and after that, a total of 24 have been helping to diminish the error. Basically, the receptor calculates the distance between itself and three or more other satellites. Each satellite has an Atomic watch and sends a C/A signal that distinguishes from others. The receptor measures the time delay for each satellite by producing an identical signal to the one emitted by the satellite, and measuring the delay between to signals so as to know the distance between them.

Without the accuracy of Atomic Watches GPS would be useless.

With this measure of the distance the receptor knows that it is on the surface of an sphere centred on the satellite with a radium of such distance.

When 4 satellites are measured at the same time it is possible to situate the receptor in a certain point in space.
